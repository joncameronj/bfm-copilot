# Thyroid Lab Interpretation Guide

## Overview

This guide provides detailed interpretation of thyroid lab markers using both conventional and functional/optimal ranges. Understanding the patterns across multiple markers is essential for accurate diagnosis.

## Individual Marker Interpretation

### TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

**What it measures**: Pituitary response to circulating thyroid hormones

| Level | Conventional | Optimal | Interpretation |
|-------|--------------|---------|----------------|
| Very Low | < 0.1 | < 0.5 | Hyperthyroid or over-medication |
| Low | 0.1-0.45 | 0.5-1.0 | Possible subclinical hyperthyroid |
| Optimal | 0.45-4.5 | 1.0-2.0 | Normal thyroid function |
| Elevated | 4.5-10 | > 2.0 | Subclinical hypothyroid |
| Very High | > 10 | > 4.0 | Overt hypothyroidism |

**Clinical Notes**:
- TSH has a diurnal pattern - highest in early morning
- Stress, illness, and medications can affect TSH
- TSH alone is insufficient for diagnosis

### Free T4 (Thyroxine)

**What it measures**: Unbound, active T4 hormone

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| Low (< 0.82 ng/dL) | Hypothyroidism, pituitary dysfunction |
| Low-normal (0.82-1.0 ng/dL) | Suboptimal, may need support |
| Optimal (1.0-1.5 ng/dL) | Healthy thyroid production |
| High (> 1.77 ng/dL) | Hyperthyroidism, over-medication |

### Free T3 (Triiodothyronine)

**What it measures**: The active thyroid hormone at the cellular level

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| Low (< 2.0 pg/mL) | Severe hypothyroidism, poor conversion |
| Low-normal (2.0-3.0 pg/mL) | Suboptimal, conversion issues possible |
| Optimal (3.0-4.0 pg/mL) | Good cellular thyroid function |
| High (> 4.4 pg/mL) | Hyperthyroidism, T3 over-supplementation |

**Clinical Notes**:
- Free T3 is the most important marker for symptoms
- Low Free T3 with normal Free T4 = conversion problem

### Reverse T3 (rT3)

**What it measures**: Inactive form of T3, acts as a brake on metabolism

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| Low (< 10 ng/dL) | Rare, possible iodine deficiency |
| Optimal (10-15 ng/dL) | Normal metabolism |
| Elevated (15-20 ng/dL) | Stress response, conversion shunting |
| High (> 20 ng/dL) | Significant conversion problem |

**Clinical Notes**:
- Calculate Free T3/Reverse T3 ratio (optimal > 20)
- Elevated rT3 common with: chronic stress, inflammation, calorie restriction, illness

### TPO Antibodies (Thyroid Peroxidase)

**What it measures**: Autoimmune attack on thyroid enzyme

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| Negative (< 34 IU/mL) | No autoimmune activity detected |
| Low positive (34-100) | Early or mild autoimmunity |
| Moderate (100-500) | Active autoimmune process |
| High (> 500) | Significant Hashimoto's activity |

**Clinical Notes**:
- Positive TPO = Hashimoto's thyroiditis diagnosis
- Levels often decrease with treatment
- Gluten elimination often reduces antibodies

### Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TgAb)

**What it measures**: Autoimmune attack on thyroglobulin protein

| Level | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| Negative (< 0.9 IU/mL) | No autoimmune activity |
| Positive (> 0.9) | Indicates autoimmune thyroid disease |

## Pattern Recognition

### Pattern 1: Primary Hypothyroidism
- TSH: High (> 4.5)
- Free T4: Low or low-normal
- Free T3: Low or low-normal
- **Action**: Initiate thyroid hormone replacement

### Pattern 2: Hashimoto's Thyroiditis
- TSH: Variable (may be normal early)
- TPO Ab: Elevated
- TgAb: Often elevated
- **Action**: Address autoimmunity, monitor for progression

### Pattern 3: Conversion Problem
- TSH: Normal or slightly elevated
- Free T4: Normal or high-normal
- Free T3: Low
- Reverse T3: Elevated
- **Action**: Address root cause (stress, inflammation, iron), consider T3

### Pattern 4: Subclinical Hypothyroidism
- TSH: Mildly elevated (4.5-10)
- Free T4: Normal
- Free T3: Normal or low-normal
- **Action**: Treat if symptomatic, optimize cofactors

### Pattern 5: Central Hypothyroidism
- TSH: Low or normal
- Free T4: Low
- Free T3: Low
- **Action**: Evaluate pituitary function

## Cofactors to Evaluate

Thyroid function depends on adequate levels of:

| Nutrient | Optimal Level | Role |
|----------|---------------|------|
| Ferritin | 70-90 ng/mL | T4 synthesis, conversion |
| Vitamin D | 60-80 ng/mL | Immune modulation |
| Selenium | 100-150 ng/mL | T4 to T3 conversion, reduces TPO |
| Zinc | 80-120 mcg/dL | T3 receptor binding |
| Iodine | 100-199 mcg/L (urine) | Thyroid hormone production |
| B12 | > 500 pg/mL | Often deficient with autoimmune thyroid |
